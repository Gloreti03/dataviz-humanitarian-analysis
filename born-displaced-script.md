Born Displaced: The Silent Crisis Killing Children We Never See
An IFRC Data & Analytics Unit Campaign — 2023

The world we inherited
In 2010, approximately 41 million people were forcibly displaced worldwide. By the end of 2022, that number had more than doubled: 108.4 million people were displaced by war, persecution, violence and human rights abuses — the largest figure ever recorded in UNHCR's history, and the biggest single-year increase ever measured.
To understand what this means in human terms: more than 1 in every 74 people on Earth has been forced to flee. Almost 90 percent of them live in low- and middle-income countries.
This is not a refugee crisis. It is a civilizational failure.

Children are not statistics — but the statistics are about children
Children account for 41 percent of all forcibly displaced people, despite making up only 30 percent of the world's population. Between 2018 and 2022, an estimated 1.9 million children were born as refugees — approximately 385,000 every year.
They are born into camps, into transit zones, into countries that did not ask for them and cannot always protect them. And they die at rates that the rest of the world has quietly decided to accept.
Children living in fragile or conflict-affected settings are almost three times more likely to die before their fifth birthday than children elsewhere. In 2022, around 4.9 million children died before reaching age five — one every six seconds. Most of these deaths were preventable.

The crises the world forgot
The war in Ukraine in 2022 rightly shocked the world. The full-scale invasion of Ukraine led to the largest forced displacement crisis in terms of magnitude and velocity in 2022. But while cameras followed one war, dozens of others continued in silence.
In Afghanistan, child malnutrition cases at ICRC-supported hospitals rose 90% in 2022 compared to all of 2021, from 33,000 to over 63,000. At an ICRC-supported children's hospital in Kabul, the number of children under five treated for pneumonia rose 55% in the same period.
In the Central African Republic — a country the size of France, largely absent from international headlines — researchers published a study in 2023 that revealed something that official UN statistics had failed to capture. A nationwide survey found that 5.6 percent of the country's population died in 2022 — a rate four times higher than UN estimates, and likely the highest nationwide mortality rate in the world. In parts of the country, 60 percent of children under five die at home and 13 percent die on the journey to a hospital — because no hospital is reachable in time.
These are not statistics. These are children who never received a diagnosis. Children who never reached a ward. Children who died because there was no pediatric bed within a day's walk.

What displacement does to a child's body
Forced displacement is not only a political emergency. It is a medical one. When families flee, they lose access to the infrastructure that keeps children alive: clean water, vaccination programs, nutritional support, and above all, functioning hospitals.
In 2021, more than 8,000 children — an average of 22 per day — were killed or maimed in conflict zones. But the far greater toll is invisible: the children who die not from bombs, but from the diseases that thrive in the vacuum left behind. Malaria. Pneumonia. Diarrhea. Malnutrition. Conditions that cost less than ten dollars to treat in a functioning health system.
The countries at the top of our Crisis Index — Syria, the Central African Republic, Colombia, the Democratic Republic of Congo, Afghanistan — are not just places where children are displaced. They are places where the healthcare systems meant to protect those children have collapsed, been bombed, or were never built in the first place.

Why we act now
Our analysis covers 2010 to 2022 — thirteen years of data from UNHCR, UNICEF, and the World Bank, tracking 195 countries. We built a Crisis Index that combines the burden of displacement with the burden of child mortality, because we believe that where children are both fleeing and dying, the need for intervention is most urgent.
As of the end of 2022, the five countries with the highest combined Crisis Index are Syria, the Central African Republic, Colombia, the Democratic Republic of Congo, and Afghanistan. They represent five different types of crisis — a prolonged civil war, a silent famine, an internal armed conflict, a decades-long emergency, and a collapsed state. What they share is this: in each of them, a child born today has a dramatically lower chance of surviving to age fifteen than a child born anywhere else.
The IFRC's pediatric hospital campaign does not claim to end wars. It claims something smaller and more achievable: that a child who reaches a hospital should not die for lack of a bed, a trained nurse, or a malaria test. That the accident of being born in a conflict zone should not be a death sentence.
