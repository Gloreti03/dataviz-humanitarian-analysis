The silent crisis killing children we never see.

In 2010, approximately 41 million people were forcibly displaced worldwide. By the end of 2022, that number had more than doubled. 108.4 million people. The largest figure ever recorded in UNHCR's history — and the biggest single-year increase ever measured.

To understand what this means in human terms: more than 1 in every 74 people on Earth has been forced to flee. Almost 90 percent of them live in low- and middle-income countries — places already stretched thin, where every additional displaced family is one more pull on a healthcare system that was barely holding.

This is not a refugee crisis. It is a civilizational failure. And the children born inside it are the part of the story we have stopped counting.

02
The crises the world forgot

One war on every front page. Dozens more in silence.

The full-scale invasion of Ukraine in 2022 rightly shocked the world — the largest forced displacement crisis by velocity in a single year. But while cameras followed one war, dozens of others continued in silence: Afghanistan, the Central African Republic, Syria, the Democratic Republic of Congo, Colombia. Three dashboards, one dataset, and a pattern the headlines never showed.

03
What displacement does to a child's body

Two ways to feel the numbers when the numbers stop landing.

In 2021, more than 8,000 children — an average of 22 per day — were killed or maimed in conflict zones. The far greater toll is invisible: the children who die not from bombs, but from the diseases that thrive in the vacuum left behind. Malaria. Pneumonia. Diarrhea. Malnutrition. Conditions that cost less than ten dollars to treat in a functioning health system.

04
The ethics · How a number can lie

The same data, told twice. One reading saves lives. The other sells them.

On the left: an honest reading. Full axis, regional colour, confidence bands, neutral framing. On the right: the same numbers used to mislead — truncated axis, cherry-picked years, causal language where only correlation exists. We show both because the choices behind a chart are choices about who gets believed.

Why we act now

The accident of being born in a conflict zone should not be a death sentence.

The IFRC's pediatric hospital campaign does not claim to end wars. It claims something smaller and more achievable: that a child who reaches a hospital should not die for lack of a bed, a trained nurse, or a malaria test.
